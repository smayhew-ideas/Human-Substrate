The Human Substrate

A Framework for Understanding AI's Unaccounted Dependencies

Scott Mayhew

The Core Insight

AI systems depend on human-derived capacities—trust, nuance, boundary sense, cultural timing, empathy—that are currently treated as free and unlimited. These capacities aren't artifacts to be licensed or owned. They're living conditions that must be maintained.

Without explicit accounting for what AI forensically reconstructs at computational speed, the substrate itself will degrade—and with it, the value of AI outputs.

This isn't about protecting creativity or compensating creators (though both matter). It's about recognizing a structural dependency that current economic models cannot see.

Why Current Frameworks Fail

IP and copyright are designed for discrete artifacts, not distributed capacities maintained across generations.

Labor economics doesn't capture ambient contribution or collective maintenance of cultural and cognitive infrastructure.

Data economics focuses on datasets and tokens—not the human capacities that make data valuable in the first place.

AI companies track compute costs, engineering labor, and infrastructure. What they don't track: substrate maintenance costs, degradation risk, and regeneration requirements. This isn't unethical—it's structurally invisible. Until now.

The Model

The substrate isn't a single thing. It's a system of interrelated capacities organized into clusters:

Meaning: Nuance, subtext, irony, hedging
Persuasion: Framing, authority cues, social proof
Social: Trust, politeness, face-saving, status
Culture: Belonging, meme grammar, idioms, taboos, humor
Emotion: Empathy, grief language, hope, shame, de-escalation
Judgment: Taste, clarity, timing
Cognition: Common sense, analogy, synthesis

Plus emerging clusters around risk negotiation, coordination, meaning over time, signal processing, and creation under constraint.

Entry Points for Exploration

The framework applies across multiple domains:

Cultural Production as Infrastructure
Artists, critics, and cultural producers maintain taste, cultural memory, and boundary sense—capacities AI depends on but doesn't create.
Economic Implications
What happens when we treat substrate extraction as cost-of-goods-sold rather than externality? New accounting frameworks become necessary.
The Trust Centrality Problem
Trust appears central in the model because it's maintained by multiple roles (journalists, teachers, moderators, scientists). What happens when those roles collapse?
Role-Based Maintenance
Specific professions maintain specific capacities. When journalism degrades, trust and narrative continuity suffer. The model makes these dependencies visible.
Governance and Policy Applications
How do we design institutions that account for substrate health without commodifying human capacities? What does sustainable extraction look like?

Current Context

This work emerges from 30 years of identifying structural misalignments in complex systems—from redesigning institutional identity for the NAACP Legal Defense Fund to managing the Gates Foundation's announcement of Bill Gates' Microsoft resignation and Warren Buffett's $36B gift.

The pattern: seeing unused capacity or unaccounted dependencies, then designing interventions that unlock scale or prevent collapse.

Recent academic work (October 2025) in data economics validates the core dependency argument. Economists are beginning to formalize what's missing—but the human substrate layer remains largely unmapped.
