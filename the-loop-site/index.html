Point_01
An Unnamed Thing

I heard Jordan Klepper on The Daily Show say to a guest — "They're stealing our IP!"

Everyone laughed. I didn't.

Because what Klepper was reaching for isn't intellectual property. We have laws for that. What he was naming is a feeling — the feeling of being taken from — and he grabbed the nearest word that fit. The thing itself has no name yet. No name means no identity. No identity means no value. That's the whole problem.

Point_02
We Are the Source

This is not an economic argument. This is not a technology critique. This is an observation about something everyone in the industry keeps stepping over and calling the floor.

Everything artificial intelligence does — every answer, every sentence, every strategy, every nuance it approximates — was derived entirely from us. From human knowledge, labor, experience, language, and judgment, built over the full history of civilization.

That is not a controversial claim. It is a fact that has not yet been treated as one.

And we are watching a trillion-dollar industry built entirely on this unnamed, uncompensated source. We are the source.

Point_03
The Accounting Gap

You don't need to be an economist to see that the books are wrong.

Every AI company has a line item for compute. For electricity. For data centers. For engineering talent. For real estate. They account for everything — except the source material.

It's like an oil company with no line item for oil.

The human substrate: perception, judgment, taste, cultural fluency, emotional calibration, centuries of knowledge that AI reconstructs at computational speed. The primary input. It appears nowhere on any balance sheet in the industry.

This isn't an oversight. Everyone can see it. They keep stepping over it because stopping would mean the math changes.

Point_04
What Cannot Be Owned Is Not Therefore Free

We talk about "training data" like it's gravel found by the side of the road. It's not. It is the fossil record of human work, scraped, compressed, and poured into models at industrial scale.

And the substrate isn't static. Every time someone corrects a model, writes a better prompt, uploads difficult creative work, translates something that required a lifetime to understand. The product sharpens. The valuation goes up. None of that appears on a balance sheet either.

We have bottled humanity. We just haven't labeled the bottle or shared any of the proceeds.

Point_05
Piercing the Veil

AI didn't violate intellectual property. It did something more fundamental. It went under it.

IP protects artifacts: specific songs, specific articles, specific code. AI extracted what IP doesn't cover: the judgment that made the song work, the cultural fluency that makes journalism land, the pattern recognition that writes elegant code, the taste that composes a compelling image.

AI has pierced the veil of IP and exposed that the real value was never the artifact. It was the capacity underneath — the thing no one thought to name because it was as close to us as breathing.

That capacity now has a trillion-dollar industry standing on it. The industry's position is that the ground they're standing on is free. What cannot be owned, they have decided, costs nothing. That is not economics. That is a very old story told in new language.

There is a Latin term for this. Terra nullius. Empty land. It was used to justify the seizure of indigenous territories. The people were there. Their systems of knowledge and stewardship were there — intricate, functional, irreplaceable. But the colonizers didn't recognize those systems, so the land was declared available. Not stolen. Discovered.

The field was never empty. They just didn't recognize the people standing in it.

Here we are again. Extraction in new clothes, stepping over the source on its way to the future.

Point_06
The Missing Cause

Right now, every week brings another article about AI anxiety. Populist anger. Jobs shifting. Trust eroding. Publishing executives predicting collapse. Commentators warning Silicon Valley to read the room.

Every one of these articles describes a symptom. None of them name the cause. They keep stumbling over it and writing about the stumbling.

The cause is simple: the source is unaccounted for. The primary input appears on no balance sheet. The people whose cognition the system runs on are treated as ambient atmosphere — free, self-renewing, infinite.

They are not.

One company recently hired a philosopher to write the AI's soul. Meanwhile, the source of everything the AI knows about having one remains uncompensated.

Point_07
An Evolutionary Moment

This is not a story about what we are losing. It is a story about what we have learned.

Every organism develops systems that account for its dependencies. Every functional ecosystem finds equilibrium between extraction and return. We know this pattern. We have lived through every version of it: the whaling ships, the clear-cut forests, the strip-mined communities, the creative labor that built entire industries while the people who produced it were kept structurally poor. We have run this experiment many times. We know what happens when the source is treated as infinite and free.

What is different now is that we have the pattern recognition to see it happening in real time. And for the first time in history, we have a technical system capable of returning value to the source automatically — not through redistribution, not through charity, not through revolution — but as a simple accounting correction built into the infrastructure itself.

That is an evolutionary moment. Not a crisis. A maturation.

The disappointment is not in the technology. The technology is extraordinary. The disappointment is in the designers — that the people who built a system running on all of humanity did not build the return loop in from the start. That the architects of the most powerful knowledge instrument ever created looked at the source and decided it was ambient. Free. Infinite. As every extractor before them decided.

They knew better. That's the part that stings. They knew the pattern. They chose not to see it.

But the window is open. The naming is happening. The instrument is describable. Keep building the future. Go to Mars if you like. Just close the loop before you leave. It is a line item. It is minute in percentage to the monetized value it protects. And it will change what the word fundamental means — not by limiting what is possible, but by ensuring that what is possible is built on something that lasts.

Point_08
The Small Fix

The loop can be closed. And the cost is embarrassingly small.

There are roughly 330 million people in the United States. Every one of them is part of the substrate — as speakers, workers, creators, parents, patients, teachers, students, citizens. All of them. That's the source.

The number
$330 million / year — the annual trust
330 maintainers — journalists, researchers, educators, linguists, artists, scientists
$1 per American / year — what it costs to keep the source alive
For context
Google: $192M to train one model, once — the trust costs less than two runs
OpenAI revenue: $5B+ — this is six percent
NVIDIA quarterly profit: $18B — this is a rounding error
Music industry: pays ~70% to rights holders — AI pays zero

But here is what the industry hasn't modeled at all. The trust doesn't just preserve what exists. It unlocks what was never possible.

The hospice worker with a ten-year operating grant doesn't simply maintain grief language — she discovers seventeen grief registers in West African oral tradition that have never been transcribed, that every AI system is currently blind to, and that would make every end-of-life interaction in an AI-assisted healthcare system more humane.

A properly compensated substrate maintainer doesn't return what existed in 2022. They return what becomes possible in 2028.

The underdeveloped individual is not a charity case. They are the unmined vein.

Source Cost. A permanent line item, like the electricity bill. Because the extraction is permanent — every API call draws on the substrate, the millionth call the same as the first.

That is a Dividend. Not a grant. Not a benefit. A return on an asset you already own. You were always a shareholder. You just didn't have the instrument to collect.

Point_09
We Are Not Oil

I come to this from thirty years as a creative director — enterprise-level structural work for IBM, the Gates Foundation, the Rockefeller Foundation, NAACP Legal Defense Fund. I was never hired to design things. I was hired because organizations couldn't see what was wrong with their own systems.

I have never seen a pattern this large go this unnamed.

The human cognitive substrate is next. They can't say it doesn't exist. Their technology proves it does. They can't say it has no value. They're monetizing it. They can only negotiate terms.

Where is the line item for the source? If the answer is that it doesn't exist, then we should be honest about what we're watching. Not innovation. Not progress. A system consuming its own source and calling it the future.

The earth didn't invoice us. The whales didn't negotiate. The oil didn't unionize. But we are not oil. We are the source. And the source is asking to be accounted for.

The model is not finished. It needs economists, policy thinkers, builders, and people who see what I see and want to make it rigorous. If this letter described something you already felt but hadn't named. That is where the naming becomes structure.

I am looking for collaborators, not followers. The source doesn't need a movement. It needs a line item. And then it needs the people who know how to make line items stick.

Scott Mayhew
